# A Matter of Perspective: How a Galaxy's Tilt Changes the Light We See

When we look at a galaxy through a telescope, the light we see has passed through clouds of dust. Dust absorbs and scatters light, especially blue and ultraviolet light, changing both how bright a galaxy looks and what color it appears. In this project, I use a computer simulation of three realistic galaxies and a specialized code called SKIRT to trace how photons from the stars within travel through their dust. By "observing" each simulated galaxy from twelve different angles, both with and without dust, I measure how much light is lost, and at which colors, as a function of viewing angle. My results show that a galaxy seen edge-on appears noticeably dimmer and redder than the same galaxy seen face-on, but by less than the simplest models predict -- because real galaxies have some of their matter displaced from the primary axis of rotation. These findings help us better translate what we see into an understanding of how a galaxy is actually structured.
